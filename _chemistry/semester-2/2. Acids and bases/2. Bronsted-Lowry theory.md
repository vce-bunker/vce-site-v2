- acid donates $H^+$ ion
- base accepts $H^+$ ion

note water can be both acid or base (l) (amphiprotic)
everything else = (aq)

**Conjugate acid**
The acid that is formed when the base accepts the H+

**Strength vs concentration**
strength: tendency to ionise (accept or donate $H^+$)

|                              | Ionisation ability                                   | Solution                                            |
| ---------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| Strong<br>$\rightarrow$      | ionises readily                                      | virtually unreacted molecules                       |
| Weak<br>$\rightleftharpoons$ | ionises in weak/poor proportions (partially ionised) | large number of unreacted molecules (fully ionised) |
**Classification of acids**
number of protons an acid can donate
1- monoprotic
2- Diprotic
3- Triprotic
$>$ 1- Polyprotic
note that polyprotic donate one at a time
1. $H_3PO_{4(aq)} + H_2O_{(l)} \rightarrow {H_2PO_{4}}^{-} _{(aq)} + {H_3O^+}_{(aq)}$
2. ${H_2PO_4}^-_{(aq)} + H_2O_{(l)} \rightarrow {HPO_{4}}^{2-} _{(aq)} + {H_3O^+}_{(aq)}$
3. ${HPO_{4}}^{2-} _{(aq)} + H_2O_{(l)} \rightarrow {PO_{4}}^{3-} _{(aq)} + {H_3O^+}_{(aq)}$

Common question: why is $CH_3COOH$ monoprotic?
- the C in $CH_3$ forms a covalent (strong) bond and is not part of the acid component binded by ionic bonds.
